Background: Signals, Music, and Sound

Sound is a vibration that propogates (travels) as a wave through a medium such as air or water. All sound waves have some frequency, and if that frequency is in between 20 and 20,000 Hz (Hz meaning cycles per second), we percieve that sound as having some sort of pitch.

One of the most basic sound forms is the sinusoidal wave, which consists of only one fundemental frequency. Here's what a pure sinusoid sounds like. You may notice that the sound isn't quite like any musical instrument you've heard before, and that's because almost all sound is not a pure sinusoid, but rather more complicated waveforms (source). Interestingly, many of the complex waveforms can be modeled by the sum of sinusoidal waves of varying amplitudes, and there exist methods (such as the Fourier transform) that can break down a complex signal into sinusoidal components.

Background: Volume and Harmonics

All of the waveforms in our synthesizer (and all waves in general) have an amplitude (the amount of energy transfered by a wave, and in our case, the volume) and frequency. However, most waveforms in music actually consist of multiple "versions" of the same wave layered over a fundemental waveform. The frequency of the fundemental waveform is denoted as the fundemental frequency, and each of the other "layers" (also known as harmonics or overtones) have a freqency that is some integer multiple of the original freqency. The harmonics usually have a smaller amplitude than the fundemental waveform.
